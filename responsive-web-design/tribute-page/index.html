Louis "Studs" Terkel

The Man Who Told America's Story

Terkel behind the mic of one of his many thousands of interviews.(Photo courtesy of Stephen Deutch, Chicago History Museum)

Here's a brief timeline of Mr. Terkel's life:

  • 1912
    Born on May 16th in New York City to Russian Jewish immigrants Samuel Terkel and Annie Finkel.
  • 1926
    Terkel's parents began running a rooming house near Bughouse Square, allowing young Studs to socialize with people from all walks of life.
  • 1934
    Received his J.D. from the University of Chicago Law School. Rather than practicing law, Terkel decided instead to become a hotel concierge and join a theater group.
  • 1939
    Married Ida Goldberg, who would be his wife for 60 years. Together, they had one son.
  • 1952
    The Studs Terkel Program made its debut on 98.7 WFMT Chicago, a one-hour weekday program in which Terkel would eventually interview such guests as Martin Luther King, Leonard Bernstein, Bob Dylan, Dorothy Parker, and Tennessee Williams, among many others.
  • 1956
    Published his first book, Giants of Jazz, featuring his firsthands interviews with John Coltrane, Louis Armstrong, Billie Holiday, Duke Ellington, Charlie Parker, and others.
  • 1967
    Published Division Street America, his first collection of oral histories. The book consisted of interviews with 70 Chicago residents of various social classes, races, and backgrounds.
  • 1974
    Published Working, an oral history collection that was made into a short-lived, Tony Award-nominated Broadway show.
  • 1985
    Won the Pulitzer Prize for "The Good War": An Oral History of World War Two.
  • 1994
    Received the Elijah Parish Lovejoy Award, as well as an honorary Doctor of Laws degree from Colby College.
  • 1997
    President Clinton awarded Terkel the National Humanities Medal.
  • 1999
    Received the George Polk Career Award. Terkel's wife, Ida, passes away later in the year.
  • 2005
    Gave a televised congratulatory speech to the Chicago White Sox organization following the 2005 World Series championship. Terkel, 93, underwent successful open-heart surgery shortly thereafter, one of the oldest people to undergo the form of surgery at the time.
  • 2007
    Made his last public appearance, saying he was, "still in touch -- but ready to go".
  • 2008
    Died in his Chicago home on October 31st, at the age of 96.
  • 2010
    The Chicago History Museum and the Library of Congress announced a multi-year joint venture to digitally preserve and make available over 7,000 tape recordings of Terkel's interviews and broadcasts. The Library of Congress called the venture, "A remarkably rich history of the ideas and perspectives of both common and influential people living in the second half of the 20th century".
  • 2014
    WFMT and the Chicago History Museum announced the creation of the WFMT Studs Terkel Radio Archive, a public repository of interviews spanning Terkel's entire career.

"Armed with a tape recorder, [Terkel] interviewed hundreds of people, producing a series of books that tell the story of the American century verbatim, and from the ground up [...] they are the sound of a nation spontaneously unburdening itself to the first person who had thought to ask."

(British Journalist Oliver Burkeman, in "Voice of America" in The Guardian)
